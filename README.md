Uber Data Analytics Project: New York City


Project Overview
This project analyzes over 100,000 Uber trip records across New York City to uncover patterns in rider behavior, seasonal growth, and trip density. The goal is to provide actionable insights for fleet management and driver optimization.

Key Findings
1. The Summer Trend
Finding: I noticed that the number of rides grows every month from January to June.
My Take: It looks like as the weather gets warmer and summer approaches, more people prefer taking Uber cabs. June was the busiest month, with about 40% more rides than January.

2. The Weekend Spike
Finding: There is a huge jump in rides during the weekends compared to the start of the week.
My Take: Saturdays and Fridays usually see 25% to 40% more trips than Mondays. This shows that Uber is heavily used for weekend outings, and we should offer extra bonuses to drivers on Friday and Saturday nights to make sure enough cabs are available.

3. Mid-week to Weekend Build-up
Finding: Pickups start slow on Tuesday/Wednesday and slowly increase every day until the weekend.
My Take: Since the middle of the week is quieter, it’s better to encourage drivers to save their energy and "shift" their working hours more toward the end of the week when the real demand starts.

4. The Night Rush (Evening vs. Morning)
Finding: Most people book rides between evening and midnight, and very few people are booking between 2 AM and 6 AM.
My Take: The "rush hour" isn't just during office times; it actually builds up from the evening and peaks around midnight, especially on Friday and Saturday. We should have the maximum number of drivers on the road until midnight, but we can reduce the fleet in the early morning hours since demand is very low then.

5. Uber Trips by Base (Pareto Analysis)
Finding: I analyzed which "Bases"  handle the most trips. I found that just two bases (B02764 and B02682) are responsible for the vast majority of all Uber rides.
My Take: According to the red line in the chart, these top two bases handle almost 60-70% of the total business. This is important because it tells the company exactly where to focus their maintenance and staff. Instead of worrying about all 8 bases, they can focus on the top 2 or 3 to keep most of their customers happy.

6. Airport Demand Analysis
Finding: I focused on the major airports and found that demand peaks late at night, with JFK consistently having the highest number of pickups. On the other hand, demand is extremely low in the early morning hours between 1 AM and 5 AM.

My Take: We should move more drivers to these airport routes during the night when demand is highest. By reducing the number of drivers during the quiet 1 AM–5 AM window and shifting them to the night rush, we can reduce passenger wait times and help drivers earn more profit by keeping them busy.

7. City Heatmap (Spatial Hotspots)
Finding: By plotting a heatmap of the pickup locations, I found that the vast majority of rides start in the center or "heart" of the city. While the pickups spread out to other areas during the day, the busiest spot from 9 AM all the way until midnight remains the city center.

My Take: This shows that the city center is the "permanent hotspot" for Uber. Even though people travel to the outskirts, the most consistent demand is in the middle. We should keep a "core group" of drivers stationed in the center at all times to handle this constant flow of passengers.
